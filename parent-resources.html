Navigating sex education conversations

Practical guidance for talking about bodies, consent, and relationships. Scripts, common questions, and resources so you can lead sex education conversations with clarity and care.

Before you start

This guide is designed as a path, not a script you have to memorize. You can move through each step at your own pace and circle back when your teen is ready for more.

  • Clarify your own values about bodies, relationships, and safety so you feel grounded before you begin.
  • Decide what you want your teen to walk away with after this talk: one idea, one safety skill, or one promise that you are available.
  • Pick a moment that feels low pressure for both of you, like a drive, a walk, or while doing a shared activity.

Opening the conversation

You do not need to be perfect. You only need to be clear, calm, and willing to listen.

  • Start with an invitation, for example “There are some things about bodies and relationships that I want you to hear from me so you never have to wonder.”
  • Use neutral language and short sentences so information feels easier to absorb.
  • Pause often and ask “Does that make sense?” or “What questions come up for you when you hear this?”

Talking about consent and boundaries

Consent, boundaries, and respect are the heart of every sex education conversation, no matter how old your teen is.

  • Explain that consent means an enthusiastic yes that can be changed at any time, even in the middle of a situation.
  • Give concrete examples of what respect looks like, such as stopping when someone hesitates, changes their mind, or seems uncomfortable.
  • Practice simple phrases your teen can use to say no or to check in with someone else.

Handling tricky questions

Your teen may ask questions about porn, sexual orientation, gender, or their friends’ experiences.

  • Thank them for asking, even if you do not have an answer yet. Curiosity is a sign that they trust you.
  • If you need time, say “I am glad you asked. I want to think about how to explain this clearly so I will come back to you tomorrow.”
  • Use questions as a window into what they already know or believe by asking “What have you heard about this so far?”

Keeping the door open

The most protective thing you can offer is an ongoing invitation to talk, not one big lecture.

  • End each conversation with clear language such as “You can always come back with more questions. I might not know everything but I will help you find answers.”
  • Check in again after media moments, school lessons, or real life events that raise new topics.
  • Remind your teen of other trusted adults they can go to if talking to you feels hard in the moment.
